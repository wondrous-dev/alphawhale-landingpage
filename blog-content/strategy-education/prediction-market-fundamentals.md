## What Are Prediction Markets?

Prediction markets are trading platforms where participants bet on the outcomes of future events. Prices in these markets reflect the collective probability assessments of all participants.

Unlike traditional betting where you're betting against a house, prediction markets let you trade against other participants. This creates true price discovery—prices move as opinions and information change.

## The Core Mechanism

Prediction markets work through a simple but powerful mechanism.

**Binary outcomes:** Most markets pose yes/no questions. Will X happen? Won't it?

**Share prices:** Shares in each outcome trade at prices between $0 and $1. A price of $0.70 implies a 70% probability.

**Resolution:** When the event occurs, winning shares pay $1.00 and losing shares pay $0.

**Trading before resolution:** You can buy and sell before events resolve, profiting from price movements.

This mechanism makes prediction markets both a trading venue and an information aggregation system.

## Why Prediction Markets Work

Prediction markets aggregate information effectively for several reasons.

**Skin in the game:** Participants risk real money, encouraging honest assessment rather than empty opinions.

**Diversity of information:** Different participants bring different knowledge and perspectives.

**Continuous updating:** Prices update in real-time as new information arrives.

**Self-correction:** If prices deviate from fair value, traders profit by correcting them.

Research shows prediction markets often forecast outcomes more accurately than polls, experts, or statistical models.

## Key Concepts

Understanding these fundamental concepts is essential.

**Implied probability:** The share price equals the market's probability estimate. $0.65 = 65% probability.

**Spread:** The difference between buy and sell prices. Wider spreads mean less liquidity.

**Liquidity:** How easily you can buy or sell without moving prices significantly.

**Volume:** The amount of trading activity. Higher volume generally indicates more reliable prices.

**Resolution criteria:** The specific conditions that determine how markets resolve.

## Types of Prediction Markets

Different prediction market types serve different purposes.

**Binary markets:** Simple yes/no outcomes. Will X happen by Y date?

**Multiple outcome markets:** Several possible outcomes (candidates in an election, teams in a tournament).

**Scalar markets:** Outcomes on a continuous scale (what will be the temperature? what will be the vote share?).

**Conditional markets:** What happens if A occurs? These let you bet on contingent outcomes.

## How Prices Move

Understanding price dynamics helps with trading.

**Information arrival:** New information—polls, news, announcements—moves prices as traders react.

**Position building:** Large traders entering or exiting positions move prices.

**Time dynamics:** As events approach, uncertainty decreases and prices move toward extremes.

**Sentiment shifts:** Changes in crowd psychology can move prices even without new information.

## Market Efficiency

How efficiently do prediction markets incorporate information?

**Generally efficient:** Major information is usually reflected in prices quickly.

**Imperfect efficiency:** Unlike highly liquid financial markets, prediction markets can have exploitable mispricings.

**Varies by market:** Popular markets with high volume are more efficient than obscure ones.

**Edge exists:** Skilled traders can outperform by identifying and trading mispricings.

The partial efficiency of prediction markets creates opportunity for informed traders while maintaining useful price signals.

## Reading Market Information

Extracting useful information from markets requires interpretation.

**Current price:** The market's current probability estimate.

**Price history:** How opinions have evolved over time.

**Volume patterns:** High volume on price moves suggests conviction.

**Order book depth:** How much liquidity exists at various prices.

**Related markets:** How correlated markets are pricing similar or connected outcomes.

## Fundamental Analysis in Prediction Markets

Traditional fundamental analysis adapts to prediction markets.

**Event analysis:** Understanding the underlying event, its dynamics, and likely drivers.

**Information assessment:** Evaluating sources and quality of relevant information.

**Probability estimation:** Developing your own probability estimate to compare with market prices.

**Edge identification:** Finding where your analysis differs meaningfully from market consensus.

## Technical Analysis Applications

Some technical analysis concepts apply to prediction markets.

**Trend analysis:** Identifying directional momentum in prices.

**Support and resistance:** Price levels where buying or selling tends to emerge.

**Volume confirmation:** Using trading volume to validate price moves.

**Time-based patterns:** Predictable behaviors as events approach.

Technical analysis is generally less reliable in prediction markets than in traditional markets due to lower liquidity and event-driven dynamics.

## Risk in Prediction Markets

Understanding risk is fundamental.

**Binary outcome risk:** Shares become worthless if your outcome doesn't occur.

**Liquidity risk:** You might not be able to exit positions at desired prices.

**Resolution risk:** Ambiguous resolution criteria can lead to unexpected outcomes.

**Platform risk:** Technical issues or platform problems can affect trading.

Risk management—position sizing, diversification, stop losses—is essential for sustainable trading.

## Prediction Market Applications

Beyond trading profits, prediction markets serve other purposes.

**Forecasting:** Organizations use prediction markets for internal forecasting.

**Information aggregation:** Prices summarize collective beliefs about future events.

**Decision-making:** Prediction market prices can inform personal and business decisions.

**Hedging:** Bet on outcomes you don't want to happen as a form of insurance.

## Market Limitations

Prediction markets aren't perfect.

**Thin markets:** Low liquidity markets may not reflect true probabilities.

**Manipulation concerns:** Large players might temporarily distort prices.

**Regulatory constraints:** Legal restrictions limit access in some jurisdictions.

**Time limitations:** Markets close at specific times and may not capture all relevant information.

Understanding limitations helps you use prediction markets appropriately.
