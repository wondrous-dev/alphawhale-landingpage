Event-Driven Trading Basics

Calendar events are the heartbeat of prediction markets. Unlike traditional financial markets where earnings and economic data drive volatility, Polymarket revolves around scheduled events—elections, sports games, policy decisions, and cultural moments.

Event-driven trading means positioning before, during, or after these scheduled catalysts. The key advantage? You know exactly when the event occurs, allowing precise timing of entries and exits.

Types of Calendar Events

Political Events:
  • Elections (presidential, congressional, international)
  • Primary debates and caucuses
  • Policy announcements and votes
  • Supreme Court decisions
  • International summits
Sports Events:
  • Championship games (Super Bowl, World Cup, etc.)
  • Playoff series
  • Award ceremonies (MVP, Ballon d'Or)
  • Draft picks and trades
Economic Events:
  • Fed rate decisions
  • Jobs reports and economic data
  • Corporate earnings
  • Crypto events (halving, ETF decisions)
Cultural Events:
  • Award shows (Oscars, Grammys)
  • Product launches
  • Celebrity news and announcements
  • TV show finales

Pre-Event Positioning

The period before an event often offers the best risk-reward opportunities:

Early positioning advantages:
  • Better prices before crowds arrive
  • Lower competition for contrarian views
  • Time for research edge to materialize
  • Gradual position building without slippage
Timing considerations:

Enter too early, and you tie up capital. Enter too late, and prices may already reflect consensus. The sweet spot depends on the event:

  • Elections: Position weeks to months ahead when polling shifts create opportunities
  • Sports: Days before for injury news; hours before for lineup confirmations
  • Awards: Weeks ahead based on insider buzz and historical patterns
Pre-event volatility:

Markets often become more volatile as events approach. This creates opportunities for traders who can handle short-term swings while maintaining conviction in their thesis.

During-Event Strategies

Live event trading requires speed and preparation:

Real-time adjustments:

As events unfold, prices update rapidly. Early results, breaking news, or momentum shifts create opportunities for quick trades.

Partial position management:

Consider taking profits on portions of your position as the event progresses favorably, while keeping some exposure for full resolution.

Reversal opportunities:

Initial market reactions often overshoot. If early results cause panic or euphoria, the subsequent price may overcorrect—creating opportunities to trade against the crowd.

Technical preparation:
  • Have funds ready and connected
  • Know the market IDs you want to trade
  • Monitor multiple information sources simultaneously
  • Use mobile for flexibility if needed

Post-Event Opportunities

Event resolution doesn't mean trading ends:

Resolution delays:

Many events take time to officially resolve. Markets may remain active while waiting for official results, creating opportunities if you have high conviction.

Related markets:

One event's outcome affects other markets. An election result impacts policy markets; a championship changes next-season predictions.

Mispriced follow-ups:

Markets often lag in updating related positions. If a primary result makes a general election outcome more likely, related markets may take time to adjust.

Building Your Trading Calendar

Successful event traders maintain organized calendars:

Essential information:
  • Event date and time (with timezone)
  • Related Polymarket markets
  • Key information sources
  • Your thesis and target prices
  • Entry and exit triggers
Calendar sources:
  • Political: Election calendars, congressional schedules
  • Sports: League schedules, playoff brackets
  • Economic: Fed calendars, earnings schedules
  • Cultural: Award show dates, product launch rumors
Prioritization:

Not all events deserve equal attention. Focus on events where you have knowledge edge, sufficient market liquidity, and attractive risk-reward.

Risk Management for Events

Event trading carries unique risks:

Binary outcomes:

Many events resolve definitively. Unlike traditional markets where positions can recover, a wrong prediction market bet goes to zero. Size positions accordingly.

Information asymmetry:

Others may have better information. Insiders, domain experts, and professional forecasters compete in the same markets. Know your edge—or acknowledge when you don't have one.

Liquidity risk:

Liquidity often dries up near resolution. Exit strategies should account for potentially difficult exits in the final hours.

Correlation:

Multiple positions on the same event create correlated risk. A single outcome could affect multiple bets simultaneously.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Chasing obvious events:

High-profile events attract the most traders and often have the most efficient prices. Edge exists in less-watched events.

Ignoring time value:

Positions held for months tie up capital. Consider the opportunity cost of early entries that take months to resolve.

Overtrading during events:

Live event volatility can trigger overtrading. Stick to your pre-event plan rather than reacting to every fluctuation.

Neglecting post-event markets:

Once an event ends, traders often ignore related opportunities. The next event cycle has already begun.

Conclusion

Calendar events provide the structure around which prediction market trading revolves. By systematically identifying upcoming events, building informed positions, and managing risk appropriately, you can capitalize on these scheduled catalysts.

The key is preparation: know what events matter, understand the markets they affect, and have clear plans for before, during, and after. Event-driven trading rewards those who do the work ahead of time.
