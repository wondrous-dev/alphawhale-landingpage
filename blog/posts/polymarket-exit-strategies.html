Why Exits Matter

Entry gets all the attention, but exits determine your actual returns. A brilliant entry followed by a poor exit yields mediocre results. A mediocre entry with a well-timed exit can still be profitable. Mastering exits is essential for consistent prediction market success.

On Polymarket, exits are particularly important because markets have defined end dates. You're always moving toward resolution, and the decision between holding to settlement versus selling early constantly presents itself.

The Hold vs. Sell Decision

Every position presents a fundamental choice:

Hold to resolution:

If you're right, you receive $1 per share. No slippage, no timing needed. The downside: if wrong, you receive $0.

Sell before resolution:

Lock in current market price. Capture profits or cut losses. Trade certainty now for potential upside later.

Neither approach is always correct. The right choice depends on your conviction, risk tolerance, and the specific situation.

Taking Profits

When to take profits on winning positions:

Target reached:

If you entered at 40¢ expecting 70¢, consider selling at 70¢. Don't move goalposts because things went well.

Risk-reward changed:

At 40¢, upside was 150%. At 85¢, upside is only 17%. The same position offers different risk-reward at different prices.

Thesis completed:

If you traded a specific catalyst and it played out, the trade is done. Remaining upside may require a new thesis.

Better opportunities:

Capital in winning positions isn't free. If better risk-reward exists elsewhere, rotating makes sense.

Overexposure:

A winning position may grow to uncomfortable size. Trimming reduces concentration risk.

Cutting Losses

When to exit losing positions:

Thesis invalidated:

If the reason you entered no longer holds, exit regardless of price. Don't hope for recovery on a broken thesis.

Stop loss hit:

Pre-defined stop losses exist for a reason. Honor them even when painful.

Opportunity cost:

Capital stuck in losing positions can't capture new opportunities. Sometimes cutting losses enables better trades.

Drawdown limits:

If a position threatens your overall portfolio, protecting capital takes priority over salvaging the trade.

Information changed:

New information may make your position untenable. Adjust quickly when the landscape shifts.

Partial Exits

You don't have to exit all at once:

Scaling out profits:

Sell portions at different price levels. Lock in some gains while maintaining exposure.

Reducing risk:

Take off enough to make the position stress-free. Let remaining shares run with house money.

Thesis uncertainty:

When conviction weakens but doesn't disappear, partial exits balance risk and opportunity.

Practical approach:
  • Sell 25-50% when position doubles
  • Sell another portion at 3x
  • Let remainder ride to resolution

Timing Your Exit

When during a market's life to exit:

Pre-catalyst:

Exit before major events if you want certainty. You sacrifice potential upside for locked-in gains.

During volatility:

Volatile periods offer exit opportunities at favorable prices—if you're positioned correctly.

Post-catalyst:

After news breaks, markets adjust quickly. Early exits often get better prices than late ones.

Near resolution:

Liquidity often decreases as resolution approaches. Plan exits with buffer time.

Liquidity Considerations

Exit execution matters:

Market impact:

Large orders move prices. If your position is significant relative to market depth, plan accordingly.

Splitting orders:

Execute in chunks to minimize price impact. Patience often yields better average prices.

Limit orders:

Use limit orders for non-urgent exits. Market orders in thin books cause slippage.

Time of day:

Liquidity varies. Major markets often have better depth during active trading hours.

Emotional Discipline

Emotions often sabotage exits:

Greed:

"It's going higher" often precedes losses. Take reasonable profits rather than chasing maximum gains.

Fear:

Panic selling at lows locks in unnecessary losses. Stick to your plan unless fundamentals changed.

Hope:

"It'll come back" keeps you in positions you should exit. Hope isn't a strategy.

Regret avoidance:

Fear of selling before the top prevents any selling. Perfect exits don't exist—good exits do.

Combat emotions with rules:
  • Pre-define exit criteria before entering
  • Write down your plan and follow it
  • Review exits to learn, not to beat yourself up

Exit Frameworks

Systematic approaches to exits:

Price-based exits:

Define specific prices for profit-taking and stop-losses before entry. Execute mechanically.

Time-based exits:

Set maximum holding periods. If a trade hasn't worked by deadline, exit regardless.

Event-based exits:

Exit before, during, or after specific catalysts based on your strategy and risk tolerance.

Thesis-based exits:

Continue monitoring your thesis. Exit when it's confirmed, invalidated, or no longer applicable.

Portfolio-based exits:

Adjust positions based on overall portfolio needs—rebalancing, risk reduction, or capital reallocation.

Conclusion

Exit strategy separates consistent traders from gamblers. By planning exits before entry, managing emotions, and executing with discipline, you transform prediction market trading from guessing into a systematic practice.

Remember: you don't need perfect exits. You need good enough exits, executed consistently. Over time, this discipline compounds into meaningful outperformance.

Start every trade by asking: "When will I exit this position?" The answer should be clear before you click buy.
